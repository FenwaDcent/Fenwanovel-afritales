Chapter One — Testimony: the irony of destiny

The afternoon sun of Osogbo had a way of sitting quietly on the roofs — warm, soft, almost peaceful.

Students moved in clusters across the UNIOSUN campus, some laughing, some hurrying, some pretending to hurry.

But Chioma walked differently.

She wasn’t in a rush, yet she wasn’t slow. She walked with purpose — her steps measured, her books neatly held against her chest, her scarf perfectly tied.

“Chioma, abeg, no vex o, we dey go Club D-Point tonight!”

That was Abiola’s voice, loud as always, breaking the silence of the path between the library and the hostel. Victoria, beside her, was already giggling.

Chioma smiled gently. “You two will never change. Is that why you came to school?”

Abiola rolled her eyes. “Ah ah, my sister, life is not all about books na. You too dey carry everything for head like exam. You no go enjoy small?”

Victoria joined in, “Abeg leave her jare, Chioma na pastor pikin.”

They all laughed. But Chioma’s smile faded quickly.

“If I start following every noise, will my life have meaning?” she said quietly. “My mother’s prayer will be wasted if I fail in this school.”

That day, after classes, while Abiola and Victoria went to meet their boyfriends near Oke-Baale, Chioma returned to her small hostel room. She placed her books on the bed, knelt down, and whispered a short prayer.

“Lord, I didn’t come here to impress men. I came to build my future. Keep me focused. Let my heart not go where my destiny will not follow.”

It was a quiet prayer — but heaven heard.

Later that week, everything changed.

That was the day she met Olabode.

It was during a group project in the Faculty of Management Sciences. He was tall, calm, and well-spoken — the kind of man whose confidence was natural, not loud.

When he smiled, he did it with his eyes more than his lips.

After their first discussion, Chioma noticed her heart beating faster than usual.

She ignored it. She told herself, “Don’t be foolish. You came to study.”

But destiny smiled a little that day — a kind of smile that humans often mistake for love.

Weeks passed.

They became friends.

Then closer friends.

Then one evening, by the school fountain, under the fading light of Osogbo’s sunset, he said softly:

“Chioma, I don’t know how to explain this… but you’ve been the peace I didn’t know I needed.”

She didn’t answer immediately.

She looked down, then up again — her eyes clear, her voice steady.

“If this is about love, Bode… I only believe in one kind of love — the one that doesn’t destroy destiny.”

He smiled. “Then let me be part of that destiny.”

That was how it began.

No club, no hookup, no drama — just two souls who believed love could be pure.
